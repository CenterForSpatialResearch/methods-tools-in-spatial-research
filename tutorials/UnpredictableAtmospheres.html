Introduction:

In recent years, the rapid pace of technological evolution has profoundly impacted climate science, highlighting the unpredictability of our climate against new models of technology. These technologies, designed to question deeper timelines in the future, have revealed that telecommunications companies and high-speed networks, crucial to today's digital infrastructure, often obstruct the collection of atmospheric data1. As we address these challenges, new AI models are being developed to predict weather patterns by integrating high-quality observations with historical climate data. However, these models often struggle to detect extreme weather events because they are typically designed to ignore outliers2. This unpredictability has become a space of political conflict, fostering the development of counter-modeling practices that could harness tools and methodologies from Architecture and Design, potentially leading to new architectural agendas where unreal data and models might induce real environmental impacts.

Climate science relies heavily on models3, but the complexity and uncertainty of the atmosphere's climate system mean these models must continually evolve4. It is essential to reconsider how we approach uncertainty as a condition of possibility within the complexities of weather and climate modeling5. From atmospheric parameters like water vapor, cloud cover, storm patterns, and moisture levels to ground data from antennas and radar systems, crowd-sourced databases, open-source information, and APIs—there are numerous sources that can reveal spatial correlations in diverse datasets6. By developing new aesthetics and languages of prediction, we can transform concrete aspects of climate into data and images that help remap our concept of unpredictability.

Counter-modeling, analogous to counter-mapping or counter-cartography, involves creating or using models that challenge conventional practices. This approach questions the assumptions, methodologies, or interpretations of standard models, aiming to highlight overlooked perspectives, address biases in traditional models, or explore alternative scenarios often neglected by conventional approaches. When models make predictions about worldly matters—such as climate and technology—people pay attention. For every action or development, there is a counterpart of radical experimentation that challenges and reshapes our understanding of what is possible.

Recent studies and scientific publications have highlighted the uncertainty of climate models, which integrate historical data with present-day observations to make predictions. However, questions arise about how these models might break down in the future, as every model inherently reflects choices about which elements to include and which to omit7. Discussing climate prediction brings up terms like "scale" and "models," inherently linked to design and architectural practices. Measuring this technologically significant space requires a methodological approach and precision. The architecture of forecasting is crucial for providing accurate and timely weather information, and collecting essential weather and climate data requires sophisticated technologies and data models.

As of the end of 2023, a significant development in the weather community was IBM and NASA's collaboration to build an AI foundation model for weather and climate8. The goal is to improve the speed, accuracy, and accessibility of weather forecasting and other climate applications. However, while new developments in math or computing enable progress, it’s easy to overestimate the power and elegance of modeling. So, how are models actually built? How much should we trust them, and why? The power of these models lies in their ability to generate predictions or original content with limited instructions from humans. The essential ingredient is data, something NASA has in abundance. Weather forecasting agencies now use several deep-learning models called AI emulators to generate forecasts based on historical weather patterns. As a result, AI weather prediction has emerged as a legitimate competitor to conventional models. However, the past is not always a great predictor of the future, especially with the rapid warming of the climate. Current AI models often miss extreme events due to their tendency to ignore outliers, a known problem for AI models that minimize the chance of big mistakes but consequently can miss extreme events2.

Weather forecasting accuracy has seen significant improvements in recent years, thanks to advancements in technology, particularly satellite observations. Nearly 99% of weather observations that supercomputers receive today come from satellites, with about 90% assimilated into computer weather models using complex algorithms. These numerical weather prediction models convert observations into a numerical representation of the atmosphere and advance it forward in time to make weather predictions. In this framework to visualize the uncertainty of data, NASA is currently committing to building an inclusive open science community over the next decade9. Open-source science aims to share software, data, and knowledge (algorithms, papers, documents, ancillary information) as early as possible in the scientific process. NASA's Earth Science Data Systems (ESDS) Program ensures that these data are fully available to any user for any purpose, promoting and facilitating the open sharing of all metadata, documentation, models, images, and research results, along with the source code used to generate, manipulate, and analyze these data. NASA's complex database allows collaboration among many established disciplines. Over the past decade, NASA's open-source codes have been utilized in over 1,250 projects, with a significant increase in the number of these projects occurring in the last year10. Thanks to these open-source codes10, retrieving images from optical and radar satellites has become a new resource. All these data, numbers, and images encoded in numbers are accessible on the web and the cloud, in this sort of cyber atmosphere. We are empowered to access this information, but most of the time, we don’t have the knowledge to decode these data, which are encoded in complex structures. These critical dimensions of computational design and counter climate model practice try to crumble conventions and experiment with more radical tools to envision this space.

Tutorial:

Among the various weather indicators that NOAA and NASA collect and model, Precipitable Water (PWAT) is a crucial variable. PWAT measures the total amount of water vapor in the entire atmosphere above a specific location on Earth's surface. Essentially, it represents the potential amount of water that could be extracted from the atmosphere as rain or snow. When PWAT values are high, the atmosphere is rich with moisture, making it possible for storms to produce large amounts of precipitation. Conversely, low PWAT values indicate a drier atmosphere, reducing the likelihood of significant rainfall.

It’s essential to remember that PWAT by itself doesn’t determine whether precipitation will happen—it only shows the potential for it, depending on the occurrence of other atmospheric processes like thunderstorms. However, it's still a critical measurement collected by satellite instruments and is heavily used in NOAA’s weather forecasting.
